YES, and the estimate is solid

Treated customers spent more per order, while the two safety metrics — whether people buy at all (conversion) and whether they come back within 7 days (repeat purchase) — barely moved. So the gain didn't come at the cost of losing customers. We also corrected for small pre-existing differences between the two groups, which made our estimate about 13% sharper — like getting a bigger study for free.

The bottom line
How confident can we be — and when does the decision change?
NEED MORE DATA

If the change actually reduces spending by around 5%, customers spend about R$7.56 less per order (we're 95% sure the true loss is R$5–10). The whole range is below zero, so this isn't noise — the change hurts. Don't ship it.

If the real effect is near zero, our estimate (+R$0.54) could just as easily be slightly negative or positive — the range straddles zero. We cannot tell whether it helps at this sample size. The honest move is to collect more data, not approve or reject.

If the change lifts spending by about 5%, customers spend R$8.63 more per order and the entire 95% range (R$6–11) is comfortably above zero. The two safety metrics didn't move. Ship it.

The bottom line
Was our study even big enough to catch a real effect?
Yes — for effects above ~R$3.20

With about 49,000 customers in each group, our study could reliably spot a true lift of roughly R$3.20 or more (an 80% chance of catching it if it's real). Anything smaller than that can slip through looking "inconclusive." That's the key reason a near-zero result means "we couldn't tell" — not "there's no effect." What this answers: could a real-world event give us a causal check — and is that natural experiment trustworthy? (Honestly: no.)

The bottom line
Can a real-world event prove the change actually caused the lift?
No — we reject this test honestly

We hoped the 2018 truckers' strike could act as a natural experiment. It failed two of four trust checks: the two regions were already drifting apart before the strike (only a 1.8% chance that's coincidence), and just 3,604 orders were affected — too few to be reliable. Reporting "we couldn't trust this, so we won't claim a number" is the deliverable — a credible analyst says no when the evidence isn't there.
